CHAPTER I

Introduction

Throughout history, religious traditions have spoken about a future time when injustice will end and divine justice will be established. Jewish and Christian scriptures contain extensive descriptions of a promised savior who will restore moral order and peace.

The book discussed here focuses mainly on these Biblical sources, especially their portrayal of Jesus as the Messiah. It then examines how Islam understands these same expectations and connects them to the belief in Imam Mahdi.

CHAPTER II

The Promised Savior in Jewish Scriptures

Jewish religious texts, including the Torah, Zabur (Psalms), and writings of the Prophets, repeatedly refer to a future righteous leader who will transform the world.

"The righteous will inherit the earth and that God's final judgment will bring lasting peace."

These texts describe a figure who will:

  • Establish justice and end oppression
  • Restore peace and security
  • Protect the oppressed and faithful
  • Rule with divine guidance

Books such as Psalms, Isaiah, and Daniel emphasize that the righteous will inherit the earth and that God's final judgment will bring lasting peace.

CHAPTER IV

How Islam Interprets This Promise

After reviewing Jewish and Christian sources, the book turns to Islam. Islamic belief presents a continuation of this divine promise through its own framework.

Islam teaches that:

  • The promise of final justice continues into Islamic belief
  • The ultimate leader of this final age is Imam Mahdi
  • Jesus returns again, but as a supporter of the final divine mission

This is not presented as a rejection of Jesus, but as a different understanding of leadership and fulfillment within the same end-time narrative.

CHAPTER VI

Addressing the Question of Copying

A common question raised is whether Islam simply copied these ideas from earlier scriptures. The book provides a thoughtful response to this challenge.

The book responds that:

  • Similarity does not automatically mean copying
  • Later religions often reaffirm earlier teachings
  • Islamic sources reorganize and reinterpret the idea rather than repeating it word for word

The argument is one of continuity and development, not imitation.

CHAPTER VII

The Concept of Occultation

Islam introduces the concept that Imam Mahdi was born but is hidden until the right time. This idea, called occultation, is unique to Islamic theology.

The book does not claim this concept exists explicitly in the Bible, but notes that divine plans unfolding over time are a recurring theme in earlier scriptures.

CHAPTER VIII

Common Questions

Is the book mainly about Islam?

No. The book primarily analyzes Jewish and Christian texts and later compares them with Islamic belief.

Does the book deny Jesus's role?

No. Jesus is central in Christian belief and is affirmed as returning in Islamic belief.

Are Jesus and Imam Mahdi the same person?

No. They are different figures with complementary roles in Islamic belief.

Why do these religions share similar expectations?

The book argues this reflects a shared religious heritage and human hope for justice.

Does belief in Imam Mahdi promote passivity?

No. The book emphasizes ethical responsibility and opposition to injustice.

CONCLUSION

A Shared Hope

The book presents a careful study of Jewish and Christian scriptures about the promised savior, focusing on Jesus as the Messiah. It then explains how Islam understands these same expectations through the belief in Imam Mahdi.

Rather than presenting competing stories, the book frames these beliefs as connected interpretations of a single hope: that injustice is temporary and that history ultimately moves toward divine justice and peace.
